Classification: CONFIDENTIAL
Date: November 12, 2025
From: Dr. Helena Voss, Lead Researcher
To: Executive Committee

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Project Chrysalis has completed Phase 3 clinical trials for the synthetic enzyme compound C-729, designated "Aurazyme." Results indicate a 73.4% efficacy rate in reversing idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis in subjects with the HLA-DRB1*15:01 allele variant. However, unexpected side effects were observed in 12.7% of test subjects, including:

- Transient synesthesia (reported as "tasting colors" and "hearing textures")
- Temporary alterations in REM sleep architecture (average reduction of 41 minutes per cycle)
- Elevated serum levels of neurokinin B (averaging 8.3 pg/mL above baseline)

Notably, subjects with the CYP2D6*4/*4 poor metabolizer genotype showed zero therapeutic response but also zero adverse effects, suggesting a metabolic activation pathway distinct from our hypothesized mechanism.

CONTROVERSIAL FINDINGS

Three subjects (IDs: 442-19B, 442-22C, 442-31A) displayed complete reversal of fibrosis after 6 weeks, but demonstrated persistent elevation of circulating exosomes carrying miR-21-5p. This microRNA is normally associated with tumorigenesis, yet no malignant transformations have been detected in follow-up (now 14 months post-treatment).

Dr. Marcus Webb (co-investigator) has proposed the "Tethered Echo Hypothesis" - that C-729 acts as a molecular scaffold, not an enzyme, and that the miR-21-5p elevation is a compensatory bystander effect. He further suggests that combining C-729 with the experimental compound JX-403 (an anti-miR-21 locked nucleic acid) could eliminate the risk while potentially increasing efficacy to 89.2%.

FUNDING AND NEXT STEPS

The NIH has tentatively approved $4.2M for Phase 4, conditional on:
1. Replication in primate models (scheduled Q1 2026 at the Primate Research Center, Osaka)
2. Submission of full cytotoxicity data for hepatic cell lines (pending from SRI International)
3. Independent verification of the synesthesia reports by Dr. Felicia Huang's team at UCSF

COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE

FibroGen's rival compound FG-3019 (Pamrevlumab) showed only 41.2% efficacy in similar demographics and has been deprioritized. Our patent filing (USPTO 2025/11876A2) covering the C-729 synthesis pathway and the "Webb Combination Therapy" is pending approval.
